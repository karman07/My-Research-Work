### Statute

Preventive Detention Act, 1950

- Section 3 – Power to make detention orders
- Section 7 – Grounds of detention to be disclosed
- Section 11 – Confirmation of detention
- Section 12 – Duration of detention
- Section 14 – Non-disclosure of grounds

---

## Facts of the Case

The petitioner, A.K. Gopalan, was detained under the Preventive Detention Act, 1950. He filed a petition under Article 32 of the Constitution seeking a writ of habeas corpus for his release.

He challenged the validity of the Act on the grounds that it violated his fundamental rights under Articles 19, 21, and 22 of the Constitution.

---

## Issues

1. Whether the Preventive Detention Act, 1950 violates fundamental rights.
2. Whether Article 19 applies to preventive detention.
3. What is the meaning of "procedure established by law" under Article 21.
4. Whether Article 22 provides a complete code for preventive detention.

---

## Judgment (Majority)

The Supreme Court upheld the validity of the Preventive Detention Act, 1950, except for Section 14, which was partially declared void.

### Key Holdings

- Preventive detention does not violate Article 19.
- Article 19 and Article 21 are independent of each other.
- Article 21 only requires "procedure established by law," not due process.
- Article 22 provides specific safeguards for preventive detention.

---

## Invalid Provision

### Section 14 (Partially Invalid)

Section 14, which restricted disclosure of grounds of detention, was held unconstitutional to the extent that it violated Article 22(5), which guarantees the right to be informed of grounds and to make a representation.

---

## Legal Principles

### Separation of Fundamental Rights

Articles 19, 21, and 22 are distinct and operate independently. A law affecting personal liberty need not satisfy the requirements of Article 19 if it is valid under Article 21.

### Article 19 Not Applicable

Preventive detention results in deprivation of liberty, not merely restriction of freedom. Therefore, Article 19 does not apply.

### Meaning of Article 21

The phrase "procedure established by law" means a procedure prescribed by a validly enacted law. It does not include principles of natural justice or due process.

### Article 22 as Special Provision

Article 22 specifically deals with preventive detention and provides procedural safeguards. It operates as a self-contained code in this area.

---

## Dissenting Opinion

### Fazl Ali and Mahajan JJ.

- Preventive detention directly affects rights under Article 19.
- Article 21 should include principles of natural justice.
- Laws should be tested for reasonableness.

---

## Key Concepts

### Personal Liberty (Article 21)

Refers to the liberty of the physical body and broader aspects of individual freedom.

### Freedom of Movement (Article 19(1)(d))

A limited right relating to movement within the territory of India. It is distinct from personal liberty.

### Due Process vs Procedure Established by Law

| Concept | Meaning |
|--------|--------|
| Due Process (USA) | Requires fairness, reasonableness, and justice |
| Procedure Established by Law (India) | Requires only a validly enacted law |

---

## Outcome

- Preventive Detention Act upheld
- Section 14 partially struck down
- Petition dismissed

---

## Significance

This case was the first major interpretation of fundamental rights by the Supreme Court of India. It established a narrow interpretation of Article 21, which was later expanded in subsequent cases such as Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India.
